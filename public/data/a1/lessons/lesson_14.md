## Lesson 14: Wo findest du Ottos Haus?

### Lesson Theme
This lesson focuses on **describing houses and rooms, and expressing possession** using possessive articles. Through conversations about Otto's house and comparing Vanilla's house, students learn to use **possessive adjectives (sein/ihr)** in different cases and practice describing house features using detailed vocabulary.

### House & Garden Vocabulary

External features of a house:

- **das Haus** (die Häuser) — house
- **der Garten** (die Gärten) — garden
- **das Fenster** (die Fenster) — window
- **der Balkon** (die Balkone) — balcony
- **die Treppe** (die Treppen) — stairs
- **die Garage** (die Garagen) — garage
- **der Baum** (die Bäume) — tree
- **die Blume** (die Blumen) — flower

**Example Sentences:**
- Das Haus hat viele Fenster. — The house has many windows.
- Im Garten sind viele Blumen. — There are many flowers in the garden.
- Der Balkon ist schön. — The balcony is beautiful.

### Rooms of a House (Zimmer)

Common rooms and their German names:

- **das Kinderzimmer** — children's room/kids' room
- **das Wohnzimmer** — living room
- **das Schlafzimmer** — bedroom
- **die Küche** — kitchen
- **das Arbeitszimmer** — study/office/work room
- **das Bad** — bathroom
- **die Toilette** — toilet (more formal/specific)
- **der Flur** — hallway/corridor

**Note on "Bad" vs. "Toilette":**
- **Bad** = full bathroom with shower/bathtub
- **Toilette** = toilet only (separate room)

### Building Levels (Stockwerke)

Understanding how German describes building floors:

| English | German | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Ground floor | Erdgeschoss (das) | "Erd-" = earth, ground level |
| 1st floor (American) / 2nd floor (British) | erster Stock | First floor ABOVE ground |
| Basement | Keller (der) | Below ground |

**Important:** German and English count floors differently!
- German "erster Stock" = English "second floor"
- German "Erdgeschoss" = English "ground floor" or American "first floor"

**Example:**
- Im Erdgeschoss ist die Küche. — The kitchen is on the ground floor.
- Im erster Stock wohnt Maria. — Maria lives on the second floor (first floor above ground).

### Possessive Articles: sein (his) and ihr (her)

Lesson 14 deeply practices **possessive adjectives** that show who owns or is associated with something. The key possessive forms are **sein** (his/its) and **ihr** (her).

#### Possessive Articles in Nominative Case (Subject)

| Subject | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|---------|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| ich | mein | meine | mein | meine |
| du | dein | deine | dein | deine |
| **er** | **sein** | **seine** | **sein** | **seine** |
| **sie (singular)** | **ihr** | **ihre** | **ihr** | **ihre** |

**Key Examples:**
- Da oben ist **sein** Balkon. — Up there is his balcony.
- Und da hinten ist **seine** Garage. — And back there is his garage.
- **Sein** Haus finde ich schön. — I find his house beautiful.
- **Seine** Frau? — His wife? Otto hat keine Frau. — Otto doesn't have a wife.

**For "her" (Vanilla's perspective):**
- Vanillas Garten mag ich besonders. — I particularly like Vanilla's garden.
- **Ihren** Garten mag ich besonders. — I particularly like her garden.
- **Ihre** Eltern kommen aus der Schweiz. — Her parents come from Switzerland.

#### Possessive Articles in Accusative Case (Object)

| Subject | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|---------|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| ich | meinen | meine | mein | meine |
| du | deinen | deine | dein | deine |
| **er** | **seinen** | **seine** | **sein** | **seine** |
| **sie (singular)** | **ihren** | **ihre** | **ihr** | **ihre** |

**Important Changes (shown in red):**
- Masculine singular changes: sein → **seinen** (accusative)
- Feminine and neuter remain the same
- Plural remains the same

**Examples:**
- Ich mag **seinen** Garten nicht so. — I don't like his garden that much.
- **Seinen** Sohn Wolfgang besucht er oft. — He often visits his son Wolfgang.
- **Ihre** Eltern — her parents (accusative plural)
- Ich mag **ihre** Möbel besonders. — I particularly like her furniture.

### Genitive Case Introduction (Preview)

The lesson introduces the **Genitiv (genitive case)**, which shows possession and is used with "von":

**Ottos Nachbarin** = **die Nachbarin von Otto**
(Otto's neighbor = the neighbor of Otto)

This is a fundamental German structure for showing "of" relationships. The genitive is important for expressing possession in formal/written German.

**Examples:**
- Das Haus von Otto — Otto's house
- Der Garten von Vanilla — Vanilla's garden
- Die Frau von Herr Wörle — Mr. Wörle's wife

### House Description Vocabulary

Words used to describe houses and rooms:

**Positive Descriptions:**
- groß — big
- elegant — elegant
- klein — small
- gemütlich — cozy/comfortable
- schön — beautiful
- modern — modern

**Expressing Preferences:**
- Ich finde ... (interessant/langweilig/...) — I find ... (interesting/boring/...)
- ... mag ich (besonders) — I like ... (especially)
- ... mag ich gar nicht — I don't like ... at all
- ... mag ich nicht so — I don't like ... that much
- Aber/Und ... sieht toll / ... / nicht so schön aus — But/And ... looks great / ... / not so nice

**Example Dialogue:**
- Wie findest du Ottos Haus? — What do you think of Otto's house?
- Sein Haus finde ich schön. — I find his house beautiful.
- Aber seinen Garten mag ich nicht so. — But I don't like his garden that much.

### Sample Dialogue: Comparing Otto's and Vanilla's Houses

**Lena:** Wie findest du Ottos Haus?
**Maria:** Sein Haus finde ich schön. Aber seinen Garten mag ich nicht so.
**Lena:** Und wie findest du Vanillas Haus?
**Maria:** Vanillas Haus ist gemütlich. Ihren Garten mag ich besonders.
**Lena:** Ja, zwischen dem Dom und der Bank — das ist in der Nähe.

**Key Points:**
- Using **sein/seine** for Otto (masculine noun = "er")
- Using **ihr/ihre** for Vanilla (feminine noun = "sie")
- Expressing preferences with **mag ich**
- Describing houses with adjectives

### Room Layout Description

Understanding how to describe spatial relationships in a house:

**Directional Words (from earlier lesson, now applied to houses):**
- **oben** — up/above/upstairs
- **unten** — down/below/downstairs
- **vorn(e)** — in front
- **hinten** — in back/rear
- **links** — on the left
- **rechts** — on the right
- **in der Mitte** — in the middle

**Examples:**
- Da oben ist sein Balkon. — Up there/upstairs is his balcony.
- Im Erdgeschoss ist die Küche. — In the ground floor is the kitchen.
- Der Keller ist unten. — The basement is down below.
- Links ist das Wohnzimmer. — On the left is the living room.

### Important Grammar Rules

**Rule 1: Possessive articles agree with the noun they modify**

The possessive article's ending depends on the **noun's gender, case, and number** — NOT on who owns it.

❌ Das ist sein Küche. (wrong — Küche is feminine)
✅ Das ist **seine** Küche. (correct — sein + feminine = seine)

❌ Ich mag seinen Garten nicht. (wrong case)
✅ Ich mag **seinen** Garten nicht. (correct — accusative masculine = seinen)

**Rule 2: Nominative vs. Accusative changes only affect masculine singular**

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Nominative | sein | seine | sein | seine |
| Accusative | **seinen** | seine | sein | seine |

Feminine, neuter, and plural forms stay the same in both cases.

**Rule 3: Possessive articles follow the same pattern as indefinite articles (ein, eine, ein)**

- ein Balkon → **ein** schöner Balkon → **seinen** Balkon (masculine)
- eine Garage → **eine** schöne Garage → **ihre** Garage (feminine)
- ein Haus → **ein** schönes Haus → **sein** Haus (neuter)

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting feminine possessive form:**
   - ❌ Sein Küche ist groß. (wrong — should be "seine")
   - ✅ **Seine** Küche ist groß. (correct — Küche is feminine)

2. **Not changing masculine accusative:**
   - ❌ Ich mag sein Garten nicht. (wrong — Garten is masculine object)
   - ✅ Ich mag **seinen** Garten nicht. (correct — accusative masculine)

3. **Confusing "ihr" (plural) with "ihr" (singular feminine):**
   - Ihre Eltern — her parents / their parents (context determines meaning)
   - **Singular feminine:** Vanillas Eltern → **Ihre** Eltern (her parents)
   - **Plural:** Elena und Maria und Otto → **Ihre** Eltern (their parents)

4. **Using genitive incorrectly:**
   - ❌ Das Haus von Otto ist groß. (grammatically correct but less common in spoken German)
   - ✅ **Ottos** Haus ist groß. (more natural in spoken German)
   - ✅ **Sein** Haus ist groß. (using possessive article — preferred)

5. **Wrong adjective order with possessive:**
   - ❌ Das große sein Haus. (wrong — adjective goes between possessive and noun)
   - ✅ Das ist **sein** großes Haus. (correct)
   - ✅ **Sein** großes Haus ist schön. (correct)

### Useful Phrases for Talking About Houses

**Asking about houses:**
- Wie findest du sein/ihr Haus? — What do you think of his/her house?
- Wie viele Zimmer hat das Haus? — How many rooms does the house have?
- Wo ist die Küche? — Where is the kitchen?

**Describing houses:**
- Das Haus ist groß/klein/schön. — The house is big/small/beautiful.
- Der Garten ist gemütlich. — The garden is cozy.
- Das Haus hat ... Zimmer. — The house has ... rooms.
- Im Erdgeschoss ist/sind ... — On the ground floor is/are ...

**Expressing preferences about houses:**
- Ich finde sein Haus schön. — I think his house is beautiful.
- Seinen Garten mag ich besonders. — I particularly like his garden.
- Ihre Küche mag ich gar nicht. — I don't like her kitchen at all.

### Key Takeaway

Lesson 14 solidifies understanding of **possessive adjectives (sein/ihr)** with a practical focus on houses and rooms. By practicing with real-world house descriptions, students internalize how possessive articles change based on the noun they modify, not the person who owns the item. This foundation is critical for advanced German, where possessive adjectives are used constantly in daily speech and writing.

The lesson also introduces the **genitive case** (Ottos Nachbarin = die Nachbarin von Otto), which represents an important shift toward more complex grammatical structures in German.
